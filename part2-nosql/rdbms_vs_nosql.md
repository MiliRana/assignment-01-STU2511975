## Database Recommendation

For a healthcare startup building a patient management system, I would recommend using MySQL as the primary database. Healthcare systems deal with highly sensitive and critical information such as patient records, medical history, prescriptions, and billing data. In such environments, data accuracy and reliability are extremely important. MySQL follows the ACID (Atomicity, Consistency, Isolation, Durability) principles, which ensure that transactions are processed reliably and that the database remains consistent even if failures occur. For example, when updating a patient’s prescription or medical record, it is essential that the change is completed fully and correctly without partial updates. ACID properties help guarantee this level of reliability.

MongoDB, on the other hand, follows a BASE (Basically Available, Soft state, Eventually consistent) model and prioritizes scalability and availability. While this is beneficial for systems that handle large volumes of flexible or rapidly changing data, it may not be ideal for core healthcare records where strict consistency is required. According to the CAP theorem, distributed databases must balance consistency, availability, and partition tolerance. In healthcare systems, consistency is usually the highest priority because incorrect or inconsistent data could directly affect patient safety.

However, the recommendation could change if the startup also plans to build a fraud detection module. Fraud detection systems often require analyzing large volumes of data, logs, and behavioral patterns in real time. These systems benefit from flexible schemas and high scalability, which are strengths of MongoDB and other NoSQL databases.

Therefore, the best solution may be a hybrid architecture. MySQL can be used for the core patient management system where strong consistency and reliable transactions are essential, while MongoDB could be used for analytics or fraud detection modules that require scalability and flexible data structures.
